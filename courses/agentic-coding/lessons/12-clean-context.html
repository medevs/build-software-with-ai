The signature move between planning and building

Clean Context

Once the plan is written, save it, clear the conversation, and start implementation in a brand-new session. It feels wasteful. It's actually what keeps the agent sharp.

The move

Here's the habit that separates people who get consistently solid results from people who get a slow slide into mush: once the plan from the last lesson is written down, you save it, clear the conversation, and open a completely fresh session whose only input is that plan.

Not "keep chatting in the same window and just tell it to start building." A genuinely new session — one that never saw your brain-dump, your back-and-forth, or any of the dead ends you explored along the way.

  • Save. Commit or otherwise store the finished plan somewhere durable — it's your rollback point if anything goes wrong.
  • Clear. Close out the planning conversation. It already did its job; treat it as disposable from here on.
  • Start fresh. Open a new session and hand it nothing but the plan.

    • Why not just keep going in the same conversation?

    It seems more efficient to stay in one long conversation — why re-explain anything? But context is the agent's most limited resource, and it degrades well before you'd expect.

    Long before a model hits its hard technical limit, it enters what you might call a "dumb zone" — typically somewhere in the range of 200,000 to 300,000 tokens of accumulated conversation — where it visibly gets worse at reasoning. It starts losing track of details, contradicting earlier decisions, and drifting from what you actually asked for. This effect is sometimes called context rot: not a hard wall, but a steady decline in quality as the conversation piles up.

    The trap A long planning conversation full of dead ends, rejected approaches, and back-and-forth is exactly the kind of history that pushes a session toward the dumb zone. Carrying all of that into implementation doesn't give the agent more context — it gives it noise.

    A fresh session is also a fresh set of eyes

    Clearing context isn't only about avoiding degraded performance — it's also about bias. A session that lived through your entire messy planning process has opinions baked in: half-considered ideas, approaches you talked yourself out of, tangents that never went anywhere. A brand-new session has none of that baggage. Its only reality is the plan in front of it, so it builds exactly what the plan says — nothing more, nothing colored by conversation history you've already moved past.

    Analogy Think of handing a blueprint to a contractor who wasn't in the room when you and the architect argued about window placement for an hour. They just build what the blueprint says. That's exactly the advantage a fresh implementation session has.

    Why this is safe: it depends on the last lesson

    This move only works because of what you learned last lesson. A fresh session with no memory of the conversation is only safe to launch if the plan itself passes the "no prior knowledge" test — real file paths, explicit out-of-scope, a concrete validation strategy. Clear context on a weak plan and you've just handed a blank-slate agent a set of vague instructions. Clear context on a one-pass-ready plan, and you've handed it everything it needs.

    That's the real relationship between these two lessons: a good plan is what makes clearing context safe. Skip the plan and this step becomes a liability instead of an advantage.

    The one thing to remember

    Save the plan, clear the conversation, implement in a fresh session. A model degrades long before its official limit, and a fresh session is unbiased eyes that build exactly what the plan says — but only a plan that already passes the "no prior knowledge" test makes that reset safe.
