Two postures, one steering wheel

Driver's Seat vs. Passenger

You can hand an agent 100% of the typing and still be the one in control — or you can hand it 100% of the thinking too. Only one of those ends well.

Same tool, two very different rides

Picture two people using the exact same AI coding agent on the exact same afternoon. The first types a short request, watches code scroll by, glances at it, and pastes it into the project. If something looks off, they shrug and ask the agent to "fix it" again. The second person also lets the agent write every line — but before any code appears, they've decided what "done" looks like, sketched the approach, and lined up a way to check the result. Same amount of typing done by the agent. Wildly different outcomes.

That difference is the subject of this lesson, and it's one of the most important ideas in the whole course. We call the first posture along for the ride and the second the driver's seat.

Along for the ride: prompt and hope

The along-for-the-ride posture has a nickname you've probably heard: vibe coding. You describe roughly what you want, the agent generates something, and you accept it with little scrutiny. There's no plan written down, no context gathered about how the rest of the project works, and no real check at the end beyond "it looks fine" or "it ran once."

Vibe coding isn't evil — it's just a tool suited to a narrow job. Spinning up a one-off script, testing an idea over a weekend, or throwing together a disposable prototype nobody will ever touch again? Go for it. Prompt and hope. Nothing real is riding on the outcome.

Where it breaks The moment the code matters — it ships to users, it handles real data, someone else has to maintain it next month — vibe coding turns dangerous. Left with no plan and no constraints, an agent will happily invent its own assumptions about what you meant. It tends to produce code that is over-engineered in the wrong places, under-tested in the important ones, and quietly wrong in ways that don't show up until later.

The driver's seat: same delegation, different ownership

Here's the part that surprises people: being in the driver's seat has nothing to do with how much of the typing you do. You can delegate every single keystroke to the agent and still be completely in control. Think of a driver who has never touched the engine — they still decide the destination, choose the route, and watch the road. The mechanic under the hood does the labor; the driver still owns where the car goes.

In coding terms, being in the driver's seat means you own three things, no matter who's typing:

What to build

You define the intent and the shape of "done" before any code gets written — not the agent guessing at it.

How to build it

You set the approach and the important constraints, even if the agent proposes options and you just pick and adjust.

And the third, easy to forget: how you'll prove it works. Before you accept the result, you decide what checking it even means — running it, testing it, reading it closely, whatever fits the task.

Put those three together and you get a simple shape: a little bit of thinking before the agent writes code, and a little bit of checking after it does. The agent's work sits sandwiched between two slices of bread that are entirely yours.

The key move Delegating 100% of the coding is not vibe coding — as long as you never delegate the planning or the validation that surround it. Give up either slice of the sandwich, and you've quietly slid back into along-for-the-ride, no matter how confident the output looks.

Why this distinction runs the whole course

Every method taught from here forward — how to plan a piece of work, how to hand an agent the right context, how to check what it hands back — exists to make the driver's seat easy to hold onto, even under time pressure. None of it asks you to write more code yourself. All of it asks you to stay deliberate about intent and about proof.

The one thing to remember

Delegate the typing completely; never delegate the intent or the check. That single split is what keeps you in the driver's seat no matter how much of the coding the agent does.
