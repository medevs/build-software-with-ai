Look before you leap

Research First

Before you plan a task, make sure you and the agent actually understand what you're planning. Research is the step that keeps the plan from being built on guesses.

What "research" means here

Research, the R in the core loop, is simply this: before you sit down to plan a task, build the understanding that task actually needs. That might mean reading the relevant part of the codebase, checking how a similar feature was built elsewhere in the project, confirming a business rule, or just getting clear on what "done" should look like.

It's the least glamorous step and the easiest one to skip â€” which is exactly why it causes so many downstream problems when it's skipped at the wrong moment.

Plain-language version This is just "look before you leap." Don't start writing a plan for a task until you actually understand the ground it stands on.

Research is optional â€” and it scales with novelty

Unlike the other three steps, research isn't something you always do in full. How much of it you need scales directly with how new or unclear the task is:

Skip it (or keep it light)

The task is already well understood â€” you've built something like it before, the codebase area is familiar, and the requirements are crisp. Jump straight to planning.

Do it properly

The task touches unfamiliar code, a new domain, or a vague request ("make search better"). Spend real time here â€” reading code, asking questions, mapping what exists â€” before you plan anything.

Treat research as a dial, not a switch. A task you've done ten times before might need thirty seconds of double-checking. A task in a part of the codebase you've never touched might need real, focused exploration â€” reading the actual files involved, not just skimming a summary.

What good research produces

Research isn't about writing a report â€” it's about arriving at the planning conversation with the right facts already in hand: which files are involved, what patterns the codebase already uses, what constraints exist (a rule you can't break, a data shape you must match), and what "success" concretely looks like for this task.

Bring that understanding into the planning conversation and the plan that comes out the other side will be grounded in reality rather than guesswork. Skip it on a task that actually needed it, and you'll often only discover the missing understanding mid-implementation â€” the most expensive place to discover it.

A quick gut-check Before you start planning, ask yourself: "Could I explain, in one or two sentences, exactly what part of the system this touches and why?" If the honest answer is no, that's your signal to research first.

Research at the task level vs. research earlier on

You may have already done some research well before this point â€” when the bigger piece of work this task came from was first scoped out. That's a different, larger-scale pass. The research step inside the core loop is narrower and more practical: it's about this one task, right now, and whether you and the agent have enough grounding to plan it well.

If the earlier scoping already answered the key questions, this step can be quick. If it didn't â€” or if the task changed shape since then â€” this is where you catch that before it costs you an implementation cycle.
