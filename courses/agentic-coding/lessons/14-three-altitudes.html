Module 4 · Planning at Altitude Lesson 14 of 30·~5 min

Know where you're standing before you plan

The Three Altitudes

Planning goes wrong less often because of bad ideas and more often because of ideas pitched at the wrong height. Before you write a single planning doc, learn the three altitudes and which one you're at.

Why "altitude" is the right word

Picture looking down at a city from a plane, then from a rooftop, then from the sidewalk. Each view answers a different question, and none of them is wrong — they're just different heights. Planning a build works the same way. There are three altitudes of decision, and mixing them up is one of the most common beginner mistakes when working with an agent.

The key move Know which altitude you're operating at, always. The moment you let an agent quietly answer a question from a higher altitude than the one you handed it, you've lost the driver's seat.

The three altitudes, top to bottom

AltitudeQuestion it answersWho owns it
IntentWhat are we building, and why does it matter?You — the strategic doc
ArchitectureWhat's the high-level shape of the solution?You, with the agent's help
TaskWhat exact code does this one ticket need?The agent, from your plan

Intent is the problem, the users, and the bet you're making — the product conversation. Architecture is the stack, the data flow, the boundaries between pieces — the engineering shape of the answer. Task is the actual line-by-line work: writing a function, wiring a route, passing a test. Each altitude sits on top of the one below it — a task only makes sense once the architecture is set, and an architecture only makes sense once the intent is clear.

The mistake this prevents

Beginners tend to make one of two errors. The first is skipping straight to task altitude: opening an agent and asking it to "build the feature" with no intent or architecture behind it. The agent will happily invent both on your behalf, usually guessing wrong, and you won't find out until the code is already written. The second error is the opposite: getting stuck re-litigating intent (should we even build this?) in the middle of writing a single function, which stalls small, reversible work with big, slow questions it doesn't need.

Watch for this If you catch yourself deciding "what should this product even do" while looking at a single ticket, you've drifted up to intent altitude mid-task. Zoom back out, answer it at the right level, then come back down.

The fix is simple to say and takes practice to feel natural: plan wide at the top two altitudes, across the whole piece of work, and then split narrow to implement one small slice at a time. You commit to intent and architecture once, up front — then every individual unit of work descends from decisions you've already made, instead of re-deciding them each time.

How the next lessons map onto this

Intent altitude

Captured in the strategic doc — a PRD for something new, or an epic for a feature on an existing product. That's the next lesson.

Architecture altitude

Captured in an architecture decisions doc — the stack, the components, the data flow — grounded in your actual codebase and constraints.

Only after both of those are settled does work descend to task altitude, where it gets sliced into the small, testable pieces an agent actually implements.

Check yourself

Which altitude answers "what problem are we solving, and for whom"?

An agent is asked to "just build the feature" with no strategic doc and no architecture decisions in place. What's the risk?

What's the right rhythm for planning across a whole epic or feature?

The one thing to remember

Know your altitude at every moment — intent, architecture, or task — and never let an agent silently climb to one you meant to own. Mixing altitudes, not bad ideas, is what derails most plans.

Reflect & discuss Think of a time an agent (or a teammate) answered a question at the wrong altitude — deciding "what" when you only asked "how," say. What tipped you off? Bring your answer to the course discussions; comparing notes with other learners is where a lot of the real learning happens.
