The map for everything you'll do

The Two Loops

Once a system surrounds your agent, almost everything you do collapses into just two repeating loops — one you live in daily, one you visit rarely.

Two loops, not a hundred workflows

It's tempting to think agentic coding requires a different workflow for every situation — one for bug fixes, another for new features, another for refactors. It doesn't. Nearly everything decomposes into two loops that you'll recognize instantly once you see them named.

The inner loop

Where you do the actual work. You take one small, well-defined piece of a project and run it through a simple four-step rhythm.

The outer loop

Where you improve the system itself. You step back and adjust how you work so the same kind of mistake is less likely to happen again.

The inner loop: the core loop

The inner loop is a four-step rhythm you'll run over and over, on one small unit of work at a time — a single feature, a single fix, a single improvement. We'll call it the core loop, and it goes:

  • Research. Get oriented — understand what already exists and what this piece of work actually needs before deciding anything.
  • Plan. Decide the approach and the shape of "done," on paper, before any code gets written.
  • Implement. Let the agent write the code, following the plan you set.
  • Validate. Check the result against a real standard — run it, test it, read it — before you call it finished.

    • Notice the shape from the last lesson hiding in here: Research and Plan are the thinking that comes before the agent types anything; Validate is the checking that comes after. Implement — the part you fully hand off — is sandwiched in the middle. That's the driver's seat, formalized into four repeatable steps.

    Where you actually live Almost all of your time as an agentic coder is spent right here, in the inner loop. It doesn't need to run perfectly every time to be useful — a rough pass through Research → Plan → Implement → Validate on a small task still beats no structure at all.

    The outer loop: improving the builder, not the build

    The outer loop is different in kind. You don't step into it to finish a task — you step into it when you notice the same kind of problem keeps showing up across tasks. Maybe the agent keeps missing a project convention. Maybe it keeps skipping a check you always want done. That's your signal to pause building for a moment and have a conversation with your agent about what should change in your project's setup — the instructions, habits, and shortcuts you've built up around it — so that class of mistake is less likely next time.

    Think of the inner loop as driving to work every day, and the outer loop as occasionally tuning the car — adjusting the mirrors, topping off the oil — so tomorrow's drive goes a little more smoothly. You don't tune the car every morning. You tune it when something about the ride keeps bothering you.

    The asymmetry: rare visits, big payoff

    The two loops are deliberately lopsided. You spend the overwhelming majority of your time in the inner loop, shipping one small piece of work after another. You step out to the outer loop only occasionally — when an issue is significant enough, or recurring enough, to be worth fixing at the source rather than working around it again.

    And as your setup matures, you'll notice yourself stepping out to the outer loop less and less. Early on, you might adjust your system every few tasks. Later, whole weeks of inner-loop work go by without needing a single outer-loop conversation — not because problems stopped happening, but because you already fixed the recurring ones at the root.

    PurposeBuild the thingImprove the system that builds things

    The one thing to remember

    You live in the inner loop; you visit the outer loop rarely, and only on purpose. One builds the thing in front of you — the other makes every future thing easier to build.
