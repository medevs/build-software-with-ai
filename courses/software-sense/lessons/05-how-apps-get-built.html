A time machine for your instructions

How Apps Get Built

All the instructions from Lesson 1, wired together with the dependencies from Lesson 4, have to live somewhere and change safely over time. Here's how that actually works.

The codebase: all the instructions, together

Every instruction file that makes up an app — frontend, backend, and everything in between — lives together in a collection called the codebase. Think of it as the complete recipe book for a restaurant: every recipe, every prep note, every substitution rule, all in one place. When your AI "opens the codebase," it's reading that whole recipe book before changing anything in it.

A codebase is rarely one single file. It's usually hundreds or thousands of smaller files, each handling one piece — one screen, one rule, one connection to the database — organized into folders the way a recipe book is organized into chapters.

Version control: a save-history and time machine

Recipe books get revised. Someone tweaks a recipe, and if it turns out worse, you want to go back to the old version. Software has the same need, magnified: many changes, made often, by possibly more than one person (or AI) at once. The tool that manages this is called version control — most commonly a system called git.

Git is a save-history for your entire codebase: every meaningful change is recorded, labeled, and reversible.

Each recorded change is called a commit — a labeled snapshot of "here's exactly what the codebase looked like, and here's what changed, at this moment." String enough commits together and you get a complete timeline of how the app came to be exactly what it is today.

Undo, for real

If a change breaks something, version control lets you go back to the last known-good commit — a real undo button for the whole codebase, not just your last keystroke.

Who changed what

Every commit records what changed and, usually, why. When something goes wrong, you can look back through the history instead of guessing.

Why this matters for you An AI that changes code without any version control is working without a safety net — there's no "before" to go back to if the change makes things worse. Asking "is this being tracked with git, and can we undo it?" is one of the simplest habits that protects you.

Your machine vs. what users actually see

Here's something that trips people up: the codebase usually exists in more than one place at once. There's the copy being worked on — often called a local copy, sitting on a developer's or an AI's own machine — and there's the copy actually running for real users, sometimes called live or production. A change isn't real for your users until it's moved from the first place to the second.

It's exactly like a restaurant testing a new dish in a back kitchen before ever putting it on the public menu. Something can look completely finished on the test copy and still not be on the real menu yet. We'll spend a whole lesson later on these different "environments" and what has to happen to move safely between them.

Red flags to catch
  • Changes are being made with no version control at all — no history, no undo.
  • "I already fixed it" with no clarity on whether that fix is only on a local copy or actually live for users.
  • Nobody can tell you what changed between "it worked yesterday" and "it's broken today."
Ask your AI
  • "Is this change being tracked with version control, and can we undo it if needed?"
  • "Is this fix only on my local copy, or is it actually live for users yet?"
  • "Can you show me the history of changes to this part of the codebase?"

The one thing to remember

A codebase lives in more than one place and changes over time — version control is what lets you undo, track, and trust those changes instead of just hoping they were safe.
