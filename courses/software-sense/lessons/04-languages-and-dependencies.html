Languages & Dependencies

The instructions from Lesson 1 have to be written in something. And almost none of them are written entirely from scratch. Here's what that means for you.

Programming languages: different words, same idea

Recall from Lesson 1 that software is just precise instructions. A programming language is one of many different vocabularies and grammars for writing those instructions — Python, JavaScript, and others. They're a bit like human languages: French and Japanese can both order the same meal, just with different words and word order. Some languages are better suited to certain jobs (a website frontend, a data-crunching backend), which is one reason your AI will pick a particular one for a particular task.

You don't need to learn any of them to direct good software. You do need to know that they exist, and that "which language" is a real decision someone (or some AI) is making on your behalf.

Nobody starts from a blank page

If every piece of software had to be written from absolute zero — including things like "show text on a screen" or "save a file" — almost nothing would ever ship. So developers (and AI) build on top of frameworks and libraries: pre-built, ready-made chunks of other people's code that already solve a common problem. Together these borrowed pieces are called dependencies — your software depends on them to work.

Building software is more like building a house with pre-made windows and doors than carving every piece from a single tree.

A framework is like a house's structural kit — the foundation and skeleton that decides a lot about how everything else fits together. A library is smaller and more like one pre-made part — a doorknob, a window unit — that you bolt on where you need it. An AI building your app is, most of the time, wiring together dozens or hundreds of these pre-made pieces, plus a smaller amount of code written just for you.

The upside: speed

Using a well-built pre-made window is faster and usually safer than forging glass yourself. Dependencies let an AI assemble in hours what would take months to build from scratch.

The risk: inheritance

If a pre-made window has a design flaw, every house built with it has that same flaw. Your software inherits every dependency's bugs and security holes — including ones discovered after you already built on top of them.

Why this matters for you Every dependency you (or your AI) add is a small promise: "I trust this other code, and I'm relying on whoever maintains it to keep it working and safe." Add enough of them without a plan for keeping them updated, and even a simple app becomes fragile — one broken or abandoned piece can bring down the whole structure. We'll go much deeper on this when we reach the security module.

More isn't automatically better

It's tempting to think "more pre-built pieces = less work for the AI = better." Often the opposite is true. A small number of well-chosen, well-maintained dependencies is usually healthier than a huge pile of them, the same way a house with fewer, higher-quality prefabricated parts is often easier to maintain than one stitched together from a hundred mismatched kits.

Red flags to catch
  • Nobody can tell you roughly how many dependencies the project relies on, or when they were last updated.
  • A new dependency gets added for something trivial that a few lines of custom code could have done.
  • "It's just a library, it's fine" as the entire justification for trusting a piece of borrowed code.
Ask your AI
  • "What frameworks and libraries does this depend on, and why did you choose them?"
  • "Are any of these dependencies outdated or known to have security issues?"
  • "Could this feature be done with fewer dependencies, and what would we trade off?"

The one thing to remember

Almost no software is built from scratch — it's assembled from other people's pre-built pieces, which means speed comes bundled with inherited risk.
