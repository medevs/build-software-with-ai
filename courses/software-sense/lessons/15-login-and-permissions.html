Two questions your app must never confuse

Login & Permissions

"Who are you?" and "what are you allowed to do?" sound like the same question. They aren't — and mixing them up is one of the most common, most damaging mistakes in software.

Authentication: proving who you are

Authentication is the process of proving you are who you claim to be. The everyday version is logging in: you type an email and a password, and the app checks that the password matches the account. It's the digital version of showing ID at the door.

Two things make authentication stronger. "Sign in with Google" (or Apple, or similar) lets you reuse a login that a large company already secures carefully, instead of creating yet another password for the app to protect — one less password for you to forget, and one less password database for the app to guard. Two-factor authentication (2FA) adds a second proof on top of the password — usually a code sent to your phone — so that even if someone steals your password, they still can't get in without also having your phone.

Why this matters for you Authentication answers exactly one question: is this really the account owner? It says nothing yet about what that person should be able to see or do once they're in. That's a separate job, coming up next.

Authorization: what you're allowed to do

Authorization is the process of checking what a logged-in person is actually allowed to do. Being logged in doesn't mean being allowed to do everything. A normal customer being logged in should not mean they can open the admin panel, see other customers' orders, or change someone else's account — even though the app already knows who they are.

Think of a hotel key card. Showing your ID at the front desk (authentication) gets you a key card. But that key card should only open your room — not the room next door, not the manager's office. Authorization is the part of the system that checks the key card against the door, every single time someone tries to open one.

Authentication

Proves who you are. Happens once, at login. The question: "is this really you?"

Authorization

Checks what you're allowed to do. Happens on every action. The question: "are you allowed to do this, on this specific thing?"

The classic disaster

Here's the mistake that shows up again and again in real breaches: an app checks who you are but forgets to check what you're allowed to reach. Imagine an order page reachable at a web address like orders.example.com/view?id=1042. If the app only confirms "yes, this is a logged-in user" and never checks "does order 1042 actually belong to this user," then any logged-in customer can simply change the number in the address and read someone else's order. They didn't hack anything clever — the app just never asked the second question.

This is why authorization has to be checked on every action that touches someone's data, not just once at the login screen.

Passwords must be stored scrambled

A well-built app never stores your password as plain, readable text. Instead it runs your password through a one-way scrambling process called hashing, and stores only the scrambled result. When you log in later, the app scrambles what you typed and compares the two scrambled versions — it never needs to see or store your real password at all.

This matters because if a company's database is ever stolen (and sometimes they are), hashed passwords are close to useless to the thief, while plain-text passwords hand over everyone's login instantly, often for other sites too, since people reuse passwords.

Red flags to catch
  • A page or action checks "are you logged in?" but never checks "is this your data?"
  • Passwords appear anywhere in the database, logs, or code in plain, readable form.
  • Every logged-in user, regardless of role, can reach admin-only pages or actions.
Ask your AI
  • "For this feature, does it check that the logged-in user actually owns the data being requested?"
  • "Are passwords hashed before being stored, and never logged or stored in plain text?"
  • "Which pages or actions here should be limited to admins, and are they actually restricted?"

Check yourself

What's the difference between authentication and authorization?

A user changes the number in a web address and sees someone else's order. What most likely went wrong?

Why should passwords be stored "hashed" instead of as plain text?

The one thing to remember

Proving who you are and checking what you're allowed to do are two separate jobs — and every single action that touches someone's data needs the second check, not just the first.
