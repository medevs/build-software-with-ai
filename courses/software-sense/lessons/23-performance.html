The feature nobody asks for, and everybody notices

Performance: Why Speed Is a Feature

You can build software that does everything right and still lose people — because it feels slow. Speed isn't a nice-to-have. It's a feature, whether anyone put it on the requirements list or not.

What "performance" actually means

Performance is how fast and responsive a piece of software feels while you're using it. Tap a button, does the screen react instantly or does it hang? Load a page, does it appear in a blink or does it crawl? That felt speed is performance.

The technical word for the specific delay is latency — the wait between you doing something (clicking, submitting, opening a page) and the software giving you a result. Low latency means barely any wait. High latency means you sit there watching a spinner, wondering if it's broken.

Latency is the gap between "I asked" and "it answered." Everything about performance is really about shrinking that gap, or making it feel smaller than it is.

Why speed is a real feature, not a technical detail

People don't file a complaint about slowness — they just leave. A slow checkout loses sales. A slow chatbot gets abandoned mid-conversation. A slow internal tool makes employees route around it. Nobody writes "please be fast" in a feature request, because it's assumed. When it's missing, it's the first thing anyone notices, even if they can't say exactly what's wrong — they just call the app "clunky" or "annoying."

Why this matters for you When you ask an AI to build something, it will usually make it work first. Whether it's fast is a separate question you have to ask on purpose — "does this feel snappy?" is not implied by "does this run?"

Common causes of slowness, in plain English

You don't need to debug code to understand why software drags. Almost every slowdown traces back to one of a few simple habits:

Doing too much work

The software recalculates or reprocesses something it didn't need to — like re-reading an entire book every time you ask for one sentence.

Asking the database too many times

Instead of asking once for everything it needs, the software makes hundreds of small requests, one after another — like walking to the pantry separately for every single ingredient instead of grabbing them all in one trip.

Huge images and files

A photo sized for a billboard gets sent to a phone screen. The extra size has to travel over the network and be processed, and that takes time for no visible benefit.

Slow or distant servers

If the computer answering your request is far away or overloaded, every single interaction inherits that delay — this connects to scale, coming up next.

None of these are exotic bugs. They're ordinary shortcuts that quietly add up, and an AI writing code quickly can absolutely introduce them without meaning to — it's optimizing for "produces the right answer," not for "produces it efficiently."

Perceived speed matters as much as actual speed

Here's a simplification worth knowing: how fast something feels is not identical to how fast it is. A page that takes two seconds to load but shows a progress bar and some content immediately often feels faster than a page that takes one second but shows a blank screen the whole time, then everything at once. Good software manages your attention and expectation, not just the raw clock.

That's why you'll see loading spinners, skeleton screens (gray placeholder boxes before content arrives), and "saving..." messages — they're not decoration. They're telling you the wait is happening and hasn't been forgotten. A silent, frozen screen is worse than an honest, visible wait, even if the silent one is technically a touch faster.

Red flags to catch
  • A screen goes blank or freezes with no spinner, message, or any sign it's working.
  • The AI can't explain why a page or action is slow — "it's just slow sometimes" isn't a diagnosis.
  • Every action re-fetches or recalculates everything from scratch, even things that haven't changed.
Ask your AI
  • “Which part of this feature is likely to be the slowest, and why?”
  • “Is this making more requests to the database than it needs to?”
  • “Can you add a loading indicator so people know something is happening while they wait?”

The one thing to remember

Performance is a feature people feel before they can name it — most slowness comes from ordinary habits like doing unnecessary work, over-asking the database, or shipping oversized files, so ask about speed on purpose instead of assuming it comes free.
