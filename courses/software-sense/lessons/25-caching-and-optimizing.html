Not redoing work you already did once

Caching & Optimizing

A lot of speed problems have a boring, elegant fix: stop redoing expensive work you already did a minute ago. That single idea — caching — quietly powers most of the "fast" software you use every day.

What caching is

Caching means keeping a ready-made copy of something expensive to produce, so the software doesn't have to redo the work every single time it's needed. Think about snacks on your kitchen counter versus a trip to the store: if you kept your favorite snack at the store and drove there every time you wanted one, you'd survive, but it would be exhausting and slow. Keeping a stash at home — a cache — means the "expensive" trip only happens occasionally, and grabbing a snack the rest of the time is instant.

Software does the same thing with information. If calculating or fetching something takes real time or effort, and the answer doesn't change often, the software can save that answer once and reuse it — instead of redoing the full calculation or a trip to the database every single time someone asks.

Caching is just: do the expensive thing once, remember the answer, and hand out the memory instead of redoing the work.

Where caching helps

Popular, unchanging content

A homepage, a product listing, a list of country names — things that are the same for every visitor and don't change every second are perfect candidates to store a ready copy of, rather than rebuild from scratch on every single visit.

Expensive calculations

If producing an answer takes real computing effort — a report, a summary, an AI-generated response — saving that result means the next person who asks the same question gets it instantly instead of triggering the same slow work again.

Caching isn't free, though — it's a simplification worth naming. A cached copy can go stale: if the real data changes but the cached copy doesn't get refreshed, people might see old information. Good caching includes a plan for when to refresh or throw away the stored copy, not just when to create it.

A light word on indexes

A related idea lives inside databases: an index. A database is like a giant filing cabinet full of records. Without an index, finding one specific record means checking every single page, one by one — slow once there are a lot of records. An index is like the table of contents at the front of a book: it tells the database roughly where to look, so it can jump straight to the right spot instead of scanning everything. You'll rarely set these up yourself, but knowing the word helps you understand why an AI might say "let's add an index" when something is running slow.

The big warning: don't optimize too early

Here's the trap that catches even experienced builders: spending time making something fast before you even know if it needs to be fast, or before you know it's even correct. This has a name — premature optimization — and it's widely considered a mistake for a good reason. Optimizing takes real time and often makes code more complicated and harder to change. If you spend that effort on a part of the app that barely matters, or that turns out to be wrong anyway, you've wasted the effort and made everything harder to fix.

The better order of operations: get it working correctly first. Then, if something is genuinely slow and people actually feel it, look at that specific part and make it faster. Speeding up the wrong thing ten times is worse than speeding up the right thing once.

Red flags to catch
  • Time is being spent "optimizing" a feature before anyone has confirmed it's actually slow for users.
  • Cached information is shown as if it's live, with no plan for how or when it gets refreshed.
  • Nobody can point to which specific part of the app is actually the slow one.
Ask your AI
  • “Is this data a good candidate for caching, or does it need to be live every time?”
  • “If we cache this, how and when does the cached copy get refreshed?”
  • “Is this optimization actually necessary right now, or are we solving a problem we don't have yet?”

The one thing to remember

Caching saves time by not redoing expensive work — but the real discipline is making things correct first, and only optimizing the specific part that's actually slow once you know it matters.
