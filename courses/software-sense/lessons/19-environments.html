Same software, different copies

Environments: Dev, Staging, Production

Software rarely exists in just one place. It usually runs in several separate copies at once — and knowing which copy you're looking at is the difference between a safe experiment and a real disaster.

An environment is a separate copy of your software, running somewhere

An environment is one running copy of your software, plus everything it needs to run (its own data, its own settings) — kept separate from the other copies. The same code can run in several environments at once, like the same play being rehearsed backstage while a different cast performs it live on stage. Same script, very different stakes if someone forgets a line.

Most teams use three environments, and it helps to think of them as three rooms with three different purposes.

Development

Your workbench. Where you or the AI make changes and try things. Safe to break — that's the point. Nobody but you (and maybe your team) ever sees this copy.

Staging

A rehearsal copy that mimics the real thing as closely as possible — same setup, fake or copied data. You test here before anything reaches real users.

The third room Production is the real thing — the live copy your actual users touch, with their actual data. Whatever happens here, happens for real: a mistake isn't a lesson, it's an incident. Production is why staging exists.

Why "it works on my machine" happens

You've probably heard this line, or will soon: someone tests a change, it works perfectly, and then it breaks the moment real users touch it. This isn't bad luck — it's environments quietly being different from each other. Your workbench (development) might use a different set of test data, different settings, or even a slightly different version of a tool than the live setting (production) does. The code is identical; the surrounding conditions aren't.

The fix isn't cleverness, it's discipline: make staging match production as closely as you reasonably can, and always test there before the live environment ever sees a change. The closer the rehearsal is to the real performance, the fewer surprises on opening night.

The one rule that protects your users

Never test a risky change directly in production. "Risky" means anything that touches real data, changes how something core behaves, or you're genuinely unsure about. If you (or an AI assistant) are tempted to "just try it live and see," that's exactly the moment to stop and use staging instead. Production is not a testing ground — it's where the consequences are real and often hard to undo.

DevelopmentYou / your teamYes — that's its job
StagingYour team, maybe testersMostly — treat carefully
ProductionReal usersNo

This matters more than ever with AI helping you build. An AI assistant can generate a change in seconds, which makes it tempting to skip the rehearsal and go straight to the live setting. Resist that. The speed of writing the change has nothing to do with the safety of shipping it — that's exactly the gap staging is built to catch.

Red flags to catch
  • Someone says "let's just try it in production, it's quick" — for anything touching real user data.
  • There's no staging environment at all, so every change's first real test is with live users.
  • Staging is so out of date or different from production that passing tests there doesn't mean much.
Ask your AI
  • "Which environment does this change get tested in before it reaches real users?"
  • "What's different between our staging setup and production that could cause surprises?"
  • "Is this change risky enough that it needs to go through staging first?"

The one thing to remember

Environments are separate copies of your software with different stakes — development is safe to break, staging is your rehearsal, and production is the real thing your users touch, so risky changes always go through the rehearsal first.
