Directing Your AI

You now know what software is made of and what can go wrong with it. This lesson is about using that knowledge before the AI writes a line of code — so it builds the right thing the first time.

Remember the cook with zero common sense

Back in Lesson 1 we said a computer is an incredibly fast, perfectly obedient cook with zero common sense. An AI coding agent is that same cook, just faster and more capable — it still does exactly what you tell it, not what you meant. The entire skill of "directing AI" is the skill of giving that cook instructions precise enough that "exactly what you said" and "what you wanted" are the same thing.

That's not a technical skill. It's a communication skill. And you've spent 26 lessons building the vocabulary for it — requirements, edge cases, data, security, environments, cost, performance. Now you use that vocabulary in the brief itself.

Why this matters for you A vague request forces the AI to guess, and it will guess confidently and silently. A precise request gives it a target to check its own work against — and gives you something to check its work against too.

Say what "done" means, every time

In Lesson 10 you learned that a definition of done has three parts: the requirement, the edge cases you care about, and what "handled" looks like. That's the single most useful thing you can paste into any request to an AI coding agent. Instead of "build me a signup form," try:

"Build a signup form: a visitor enters an email and password and gets an account. Handle an empty field, a duplicate email, and an invalid email address by showing a clear message — don't just fail silently."

Same request, same AI, wildly different odds of getting something you'd actually ship.

Name the constraints you care about

An AI can't read your risk tolerance off your face. If data safety, security, how many people will use this, or what it costs to run matter to you — and by now you know they always matter somewhat — say so out loud, in the request:

Instead of silence

"Add a comments feature."

Name the constraint

"Add a comments feature. Only logged-in users can post. Store who posted and when. Assume a few hundred users, not a million."

You're not writing engineering specs — you're pointing at the modules of this course that apply: security, data, scale. One clause per concern is usually enough.

Ask for the plan before the code

One habit changes everything: before the AI writes anything, ask it to explain its plan in plain English — what it's going to build, what it's assuming, and what it's not handling. This costs you thirty seconds and catches misunderstandings while they're still just sentences, not code you have to unwind.

  • State the request — the definition of done, plus any constraints that matter.
  • Ask for the plan first. "Before you build this, explain your plan and any assumptions you're making." Read it. Does it match what you meant?
  • Let it build — one piece at a time, not the whole app at once (see below).
  • Review the result against the definition of done you started with — the subject of the next lesson.

    • Work in small steps, not "build me the whole app"

    A single giant request — "build me a whole booking system" — gives the AI enormous room to guess, and gives you a mountain of code to check all at once. Small steps do the opposite: one feature, reviewed, then the next. Each step is small enough that when something's off, you know almost exactly where to look, and the AI has a much narrower thing to get right.

    Red flags to catch
    • You asked for "a whole app" in one request and got a wall of code back with no plan explained first.
    • You can't point to the sentence where you said what "done" meant for this feature.
    • You skipped mentioning a constraint you actually care about (security, data, scale, cost) and hoped the AI would just know.
    Ask your AI
    • "Before you write any code, explain your plan and the assumptions you're making."
    • "Here's my definition of done: [requirement + edge cases + handling]. Build to that, one piece at a time."
    • "What constraints should I be telling you about that I haven't mentioned — security, data, scale, or cost?"

    Check yourself

    Why does a precise brief matter so much when directing an AI coding agent?

    What's the benefit of asking an AI to explain its plan before writing code?

    Why build in small steps rather than requesting "the whole app" at once?

    The one thing to remember

    Good instructions in, better software out — say what "done" means, name your constraints, ask for the plan first, and build in small steps. Everything you've learned in this course is exactly the vocabulary you need to do that well.
