From your workbench to the world

Deployment

Writing software is one job. Getting it in front of real people is a separate one, with its own name and its own risks. Here's what actually happens when something "goes live."

Deploying means publishing your software to where users can reach it

Deploying (or "shipping") means taking your software and publishing it to the production environment — the live copy real users can reach, described in the last lesson. Before deployment, your change exists only on your workbench or in a rehearsal copy. After deployment, it's real: anyone with the address can open it and start using it.

Two ordinary-sounding words carry most of the weight here.

Hosting

A computer, owned by someone else, that keeps your software running and reachable around the clock — so you don't need your own machine on 24/7. You rent space on it, much like renting a storefront instead of building your own building.

Domain name

The human-friendly address people type or click to reach your software — like example.com — instead of a long string of numbers. You register it separately from hosting, then point it at where your software lives.

Deploys don't have to be scary manual events

The old-fashioned image of "deploying" is someone nervously copying files onto a live server late at night, hoping nothing breaks. That's not how it has to work. Most modern teams use an automated pipeline, often called CI/CD (continuous integration / continuous delivery) — a series of automatic checks and steps that run every time a change is ready to ship.

Think of it like an airport security line that every piece of luggage goes through the same way, every time: the pipeline runs tests, checks for obvious problems, and only then publishes the change to production. No human has to remember every step or do it manually at 11pm — the pipeline does the same careful process every single time, which is exactly why it's trustworthy.

Why this matters for you If deploying is automated and checked, shipping small changes often becomes routine and low-stress. If it's manual and unchecked, every deploy is a small gamble — and gambles compound the more often you ship.

Rolling changes out gradually

Even with good checks, teams often avoid flipping a switch for everyone at once. Instead they roll a change out gradually — showing it to a small slice of users first, watching what happens, then expanding to more people once it looks healthy. It's the same instinct as a chef tasting a new dish before serving it to the whole restaurant, rather than plating it for every table simultaneously.

This matters because some problems only show up with real users and real traffic — no amount of testing on your workbench or in staging perfectly predicts it. A gradual rollout limits how many people a mistake can reach before someone notices and reverses it.

What this means when AI is doing the building

An AI assistant can produce a working change in minutes — but "produced a change" and "deployed a change safely" are two very different milestones. The pipeline, the gradual rollout, and the domain-and-hosting setup all still have to exist and do their job, no matter how the code was written. Speed of writing code says nothing about safety of shipping it.

Red flags to catch
  • Deploying means someone manually copying files with no automated checks in between.
  • Every change goes out to 100% of users immediately, with no gradual rollout or way to watch first.
  • Nobody can explain, in one sentence, what actually happens between "change is ready" and "users see it."
Ask your AI
  • "Walk me through exactly what happens, step by step, when this change gets deployed."
  • "Is there an automated pipeline that checks changes before they reach production?"
  • "Could this change be rolled out to a small group first instead of everyone at once?"

The one thing to remember

Deploying is publishing your software to the live environment where users reach it — and an automated pipeline plus a gradual rollout is what turns that from a scary manual event into a routine, checked step.
