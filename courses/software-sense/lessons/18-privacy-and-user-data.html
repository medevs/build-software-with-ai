You can't leak what you never stored

Privacy & User Data

Security keeps other people out. Privacy is about how you, the app's own builder, treat the information people trust you with. It turns out the simplest privacy rule is also the strongest one.

Privacy is respecting the info people give you

Privacy means respecting the personal information people hand over when they use your app — treating it the way you'd want your own information treated, not just the way that's most convenient for you to build with. It covers what you collect, what you do with it, who else can see it, and whether people can get it back or have it removed.

The single best rule: collect the least you need

The most effective privacy habit isn't a clever technical trick — it's restraint. Every extra piece of information you collect "just in case it's useful later" is one more thing that could leak, be misused, or sit around unnecessarily. The rule: only collect what you actually need for the feature in front of you.

You can't leak what you never stored in the first place.

This should sound familiar — it's the same idea as "least access" from Lesson 14, just pointed at data instead of at people or system parts. Less collected, less exposed if something goes wrong.

Tell people, and let them delete it

Two practices go along with collecting less: telling users plainly what you collect and why (not buried in dense legal text nobody reads), and giving them a real way to see, correct, or delete their own information. These aren't just nice manners — for real applications, laws like GDPR (a data-protection law covering people in the European Union) actually require this: clear disclosure, and a genuine right to have your data deleted.

Why this matters for you An AI can build a signup form in minutes and will happily collect a birthday, a phone number, and a home address if you ask for them — it won't stop to ask whether the feature actually needs all three. That judgment call is yours.

PII, and data that needs extra care

PII stands for personally identifiable information — any piece of data that can identify a specific real person, like a name, an email address, or a home location. Most apps collect at least some PII, and it deserves the "collect the least you need" treatment above.

Some information deserves even more caution than ordinary PII: health information, payment details, and anything else that could seriously harm someone if it leaked — financially, physically, or personally. The more sensitive the data, the smaller the group of people and systems that should ever be able to touch it, and the more layers of protection (remember defense in depth) it deserves.

Ordinary PII

Name, email, general location. Collect only what the feature needs; disclose it; allow deletion.

Sensitive data

Health info, payment details. Same rules, plus extra layers of protection and an even smaller circle of access.

Red flags to catch
  • A form collects information (birthday, address, phone) that this particular feature doesn't actually need.
  • Users have no clear way to see, correct, or delete the personal data the app holds on them.
  • Sensitive data (health, payment) is stored or handled with no more care than an ordinary comment field.
Ask your AI
  • "Does this form collect anything the feature doesn't actually need? What could we remove?"
  • "Is there a way for a user to see, correct, or delete the personal data we store about them?"
  • "Is any health, payment, or other sensitive data being handled with extra care compared to regular data?"

The one thing to remember

You can't leak what you never stored — so collect the least personal data you need, tell people what you have, and let them delete it.
