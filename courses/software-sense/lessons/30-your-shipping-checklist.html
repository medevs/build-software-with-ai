Your Shipping Checklist

Thirty lessons ago you started with "a computer does exactly what it's told." Here's the one page that turns everything since then into a habit you can use before you ship anything, for the rest of your building life.

You didn't need to become an engineer

You needed to know what to check, and what to ask. That's what this whole course built, one plain-English idea at a time — instructions, layers, data, security, environments, performance, cost, maintainability. None of it required you to write code. All of it changes what happens when you work with an AI to build something real.

Here's the checklist that ties it together — one line from each module. Walk it before you put anything in front of real users.

  • Does it work — including the edge cases? Not just the happy path once, in a demo. Empty fields, duplicates, invalid input — checked, not assumed.
  • Is it tested? Something automated actually re-checks this behavior, so you're not relying on "it worked when I tried it."
  • Is the data modeled sensibly, and backed up? You know what's stored, why, and that losing the running copy wouldn't mean losing everything.
  • Are secrets safe and permissions checked? Nothing sensitive sits in the code itself, and every sensitive action confirms who's asking before it acts.
  • Is it running somewhere real, with eyes on it? Deployed to an actual environment, with some way to notice if it breaks — not just running on someone's laptop.
  • Is it fast and affordable enough for how it'll actually be used? Checked against realistic traffic and a realistic budget, not just "fine for one person testing it."
  • Is it maintainable? Readable, consistent, not copy-pasted everywhere — safe for you or an AI to change again next month.

    • This same list, plus the full "Ask your AI" prompt pack from every lesson, lives on the resources page — worth keeping open in a tab, not just read once here.

    Why this matters for you Every line above is a question you're now equipped to ask out loud, to an AI or to a person, and to actually judge the answer to. That's the whole skill. Nobody needs you to write the code — they need you to know what "good" looks like and to insist on checking for it.

    The trap you now know to avoid

    Lesson 1 opened with the single most important idea in this course: working once is not the same as being ready. An AI can produce something that runs in a demo in minutes. Whether it's safe, durable, affordable, and changeable — those are separate questions, and this checklist is exactly those questions, made concrete. Every module since Lesson 1 was really just unpacking that one sentence.

    Red flags to catch
    • Something gets called "ready to ship" because it worked once, with no checklist run against it at all.
    • You can't say, in one sentence, what "done" meant for this feature before it was built.
    • Nobody can answer more than one or two rows of the checklist above with actual evidence.
    Ask your AI
    • "Walk me through this shipping checklist, one line at a time, with evidence for each — not just 'yes.'"
    • "Which line of this checklist are we weakest on right now, honestly?"
    • "If I could only fix one thing before shipping, based on this checklist, what should it be and why?"

    Check yourself

    Back to where we started: what does an AI demo running successfully actually prove?

    What should a written "definition of done" include, before you ask an AI to build a feature?

    What comes next

    You now have the vocabulary to judge software and to direct an AI well. The natural next step, once you're ready to actually build something real, is learning the repeatable system for doing that with an AI agent — planning a piece of work, implementing it, and validating it before moving to the next piece, over and over, so quality doesn't depend on getting lucky. That's a different kind of course from this one — this one gave you the judgment; that one gives you the process — and it's a natural place to go next.

    The one thing to remember

    You don't need to be an engineer to ship good software with AI — you need to know what to check and what to ask, and to actually do it before you call something done. You now do. That's the whole course.
