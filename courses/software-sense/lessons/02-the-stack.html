Three rooms, one restaurant

The Stack

Almost every piece of software you'll ever direct an AI to build is made of the same three parts. Learn to spot them and you'll always know where a problem actually lives.

Three parts, every time

Open any app — a banking app, a to-do list, a food delivery site — and underneath, it's built from the same three parts. Software people call this collection "the stack," because the parts sit on top of each other, each one depending on the one below it.

The easiest way to hold this in your head is a restaurant:

The dining room

What the customer sees and touches — the menu, the tables, the waiter taking your order. In software, this is the frontend: the screens, buttons, and text you see and click.

The kitchen

Where the actual work happens, out of sight. Orders come in, the cooks follow the recipe, food goes out. In software, this is the backend: the brain, running on a server, that does the work and enforces the rules ("you can't order a dish we're out of").

The pantry

Where the ingredients are stored between uses — flour, eggs, the last delivery of vegetables. In software, this is the database: the long-term memory that holds your data even after you close the app.

How they connect

The waiter (frontend) carries your order to the kitchen (backend). The kitchen checks the pantry (database) for ingredients, cooks, and sends the plate back out. Nobody in the dining room ever goes into the pantry directly.

Why this matters for you When something breaks or looks wrong, one of your most useful questions becomes: "which part is this?" A button that doesn't look right is a frontend problem. Money reaching the wrong account is a backend or database problem. Naming the room narrows down what to fix — and what to ask your AI to look at.

What "full-stack" means

You'll hear developers describe themselves, or an AI's output, as "full-stack." That just means all three parts — frontend, backend, database — working together as one app, the way a restaurant needs a dining room, a kitchen, and a pantry to actually serve dinner. A "frontend-only" prototype is a dining room with no kitchen: it looks real, but nothing happens when you place an order.

A pretty screen with no backend is a stage set, not a restaurant.

This is one of the most common ways a demo fools people: an AI can generate a beautiful frontend in minutes. Whether there's a real kitchen and pantry behind it — real rules, real saved data — is a separate question, and it's usually the harder half of the work.

Spotting the parts in something real

You see...It's probably...
A screen, a form, a button that changes color when clickedFrontend
"Checking if this email is already taken," "calculating your total"Backend
Anything that's still there after you close the app and reopen itDatabase

We'll go much deeper on how the frontend and backend actually talk to each other in the next lesson, and spend a whole lesson on what a database really is soon after.

Red flags to catch
  • Someone shows you a slick screen and calls the feature "done" — but nothing was actually saved anywhere.
  • You can't say whether a rule ("only admins can do this") lives in the frontend or the backend — if it's only in the frontend, it's not really enforced.
  • "It works on my machine" with no mention of where the data is actually stored.
Ask your AI
  • "Which parts of this feature are frontend, backend, and database?"
  • "Is this rule enforced in the backend, or only checked in the frontend?"
  • "If I close the app and reopen it, what happens to this data — where is it actually stored?"

Check yourself

In the restaurant analogy, which part is the "kitchen" — the brain that does the work?

An AI shows you a beautiful screen, but nothing you type is saved once you close the app. What's most likely missing?

The one thing to remember

Every app is a dining room, a kitchen, and a pantry — frontend, backend, and database — and knowing which one a problem lives in tells you what to fix.
