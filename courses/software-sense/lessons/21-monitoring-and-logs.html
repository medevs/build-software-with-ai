Knowing before your users tell you

Monitoring & Logs

Once software is live, someone has to know whether it's actually healthy — and the good news is you don't have to wait for angry users to find out. Here's how software tells on itself.

The diary your software keeps: logs

Logs are a running diary of what your software did — a timestamped list of events like "user logged in," "payment attempted," or "something went wrong here." Nobody reads a log for fun; it's the record you go digging through after something looks off, to reconstruct exactly what happened and in what order.

Think of logs like a ship's logbook. Most days, nobody opens it. But the one day something goes wrong, it's the only honest account of what actually occurred, minute by minute — far more reliable than anyone's memory of "I think it happened around then."

Why this matters for you When you ask an AI to help fix a bug, one of the most useful things you can hand it is the relevant slice of the logs from around when the problem happened. Logs turn "it's broken somehow" into "here's exactly what it did, in order."

Watching the vital signs: monitoring

Monitoring is automatic, ongoing watching of your software's key signals — is it up or down? Is it responding quickly or slowly? Is it throwing errors? It's the difference between a doctor checking your pulse once a year and a heart monitor quietly watching around the clock. Monitoring doesn't wait to be asked; it's always looking.

Alerts

When monitoring spots something wrong — the software is down, or errors are spiking — it can automatically notify a human. That's an alert: getting told about a problem instead of stumbling across it, or worse, hearing about it first from a user.

Observability

The broader idea that all this together — logs, monitoring, and alerts — gives you enough visibility to understand why something is happening inside your software, not just that something is wrong.

You can't fix what you can't see

This is the whole point of this lesson. Software that's live but unwatched can be quietly failing for hours — slow, throwing errors, or entirely down — while everyone assumes it's fine because nobody's complained yet (or the people affected simply left without saying anything). Silence is not the same as health.

Good monitoring turns invisible problems into visible, addressable ones — often before a single user notices. That's a completely different experience for your users than "we found out something was broken when people started emailing us."

None of this requires you to personally read dashboards all day. It requires the software to have these things set up at all — which is exactly the kind of unglamorous, easy-to-skip step that's worth asking about before you call anything "done."

Red flags to catch
  • Nobody knows whether the software is healthy right now without asking a user first.
  • There are no logs at all, so a problem can never be reconstructed after the fact.
  • Something is clearly broken and no one gets notified until a customer complains.
Ask your AI
  • "Does this software log important events, and where would I find those logs?"
  • "What monitoring is in place to tell us if this is down or erroring?"
  • "If something breaks at 2am, who or what gets alerted, and how fast?"

The one thing to remember

You can't fix what you can't see — logs tell you what happened, monitoring watches whether things are healthy right now, and alerts make sure a human finds out before your users do.
